Modified Social Spider Optimization and K-Means hybridization for Image Quantization

Abstract

Image quantization is a way to simplify images by reducing the number of colours while keeping them looking the same. This paper suggests a new method that combines two techniques, Social Spider Optimization (SSO) and K-Means clustering, to make this process faster and better image quantiza- tion. SSO is like how social spiders work together to find food. It helps K-Means, which can be slow, get started and finish faster by choosing the right starting points through finding the best centroids. This new way not only speeds things up but also makes the pictures look better. In tests, we tried this SSO-K-Means method on different sets of images and compared it to regular K-Means. The results showed that using modified SSO with K-Means improved image quality and made the process faster. This method could be useful in tasks like image compression, retrieval, and other image jobs where simplifying pictures is im- portant.

Keywords: Colour quantization, K-Means clustering, Social Spider Optimization

Introduction

Image quantization is a basic technique in digital image processing that serves the need of reducing the complexity of an image's colour information while preserving im- age retrieval, and various other image-related tasks. Image quantization typically con- sists of two main steps: colour palette generation and pixel mapping.

The initial step involves creating a limited set of dominant colours, known as a col- our palette. This palette is an important element of the quantization process as it dictates the colours that will be available for representing the image. The palette can contain apredefined number of colours, and these colours are usually chosen to be the most dom- inant ones in the original image. There are several methods to generate colour palettes, including popular techniques like K-Means clustering, Median Cut, and Octree quanti- zation. These methods aim to find an equal mix of colours that capture the required visual characteristics of the image.

In the next step of image quantization, each pixel in the image is mapped to the nearest colour in the colour palette. This mapping is done by comparing the pixel's original colour to the colours in the palette and selecting the closest match. This ensures that the quantized image retains as much visual fidelity as possible while reducing the total number of unique colours used. The mapping can be achieved through various algorithms and techniques, such as the Euclidean distance or the mean squared error. Once the mapping is complete, the quantized image contains only the colours from the palette, resulting in a more simplified and better representation.

We explore image quantization and introduce a new methodology by combining two powerful techniques: Social Spider Optimization (SSO) and K Means Clustering. Our ultimate goal is to make quantized image more efficient. This approach can be useful in various applications, such as compressing image, finding images based on their content, dividing images into different parts based on their identity, and analyzing the given image.

K-Means Clustering for image quantization

K-Means clustering is an extensively used unsupervised machine learning algorithm, essentially applied in data analysis, image processing, and various fields where data or objects need to be grouped into clusters based on their similarities. The core idea behind K-Means is to separate a dataset into K distinct, non-overlapping clusters, with K being a user-defined parameter. The process is initiated by selecting K initial cluster centers, often randomly. Each data point is then assigned to the closest cluster center, based on a distance metric like Euclidean distance. After this assignment, the cluster centers are recalculated as the average of all data points in their respective clus- ters. This assignment and re-calculation process is iteratively repeated until conver- gence, which occurs when there's slight change in the data point assignments and clus- ter centers.

2.1 Working of K-Means algorithm
Input: Upload the image and convert to the required scale (RGB or BGR).
Initialization: Randomly initialize the centers as per requirement of clusters.
Clustering: For each pixel of an image calculate the Euclidean distance d from the initialized centroids. Group the pixels based on the calculated distance d to the nearest centers.
Re-initialization of centroids: After all pixels have been grouped, calculate the new centroids by taking mean of all the points in their respective cluster.
Convergence: Repeat the above clustering process till we get the minimal solution or for a given number of iterations.
Clustered Image: After executing all the above steps, finally the quantized image is generated by reshaping the clusters.

Social Spider Optimization

Social Spider Optimization (SSO) [3,4] is a nature-inspired optimization algorithm that draws its inspiration from the movement and behavior of social spiders. It was recently been added to the family of nature-inspired optimization algorithms, aiming to solve complex problems in various domains, including image clustering. By mimicking na- ture's teamwork, communication, and adaptability, SSO offers a best and efficient ap- proach to image clustering, making it the most interested area for researchers seeking innovative and effective solutions in the field of image analysis and data organization.

3.1 Working of social spider optimization

Random initialization: Spiders are initialized randomly in the sample space according to the required number of clusters: Each spider represents a potential solution.

Population separation and Fitness evaluation: Spiders are classified randomly into male and female spiders. Fitness value for all spiders in the population is calculated by using objective function. Fitness values are computed based on the absolute differences between each spider‚Äôs position and corresponding pixel value.

Weight calculation and Communication: Weights are calculated for both male and female spiders and communication about weights is done through vibration opera- tor. Information exchange is the principal mechanism in SSO.

Grouping Population and Mating: The female population is assigned to a new posi- tion according to random probability factor and vibrations received. The male popula- tion is divided into dominant and non-dominant groups based on their weights. The dominant group participate in mating with female population to produce new spider. If new spider has better fitness value than worst spider in the population, new spider re- places the worst spider.

Convergence: The above process is repeated till minimal solution is obtained or for a given number of iterations to produce best centroids for a given number of clusters.

SSO is responsible for the initial optimization of cluster centers by efficiently searching for the best spots within the input space. This is analogous to how social spiders cooperatively hunt their prey by working together. Through harnessing the power of SSO, we can finalize and examine the positions of cluster centroids to better encapsulate the fundamental structure of the image infor- mation. Modified SSO is a sub part of SSO where only half of the process is used for optimizing cluster centres. The process begins with optimizing the starting cluster centers by modified SSO. Af- terwards, K-Means takes over the task of assigning data points to their respective clus- ters by computing the nearest centroid based on feature space distance. This step is crucial because it arranges data points based on their similarities, which is the key to achieving meaningful clustering‚Äôs. A satisfactory clustering solution is achieved through an iterative approach that relies on alternating modified SSO and K-Means. During this approach, modified SSO continues to work on optimizing cluster centers while K-Means works on reassigning data points based on the most recently optimized centroids. Conclusion

Had explored the productiveness of modified Social Spider Optimization which is a sub part of SSO as one of the valuable methods for optimizing centroids and producing initial centroids in the context of K-Means clustering. Our study shows effectiveness of modified SSO in efficiently finding suitable initial Centroids, contributing remarkably to improving convergence speed and performance of the K-Means algorithm. The experimental results reveal that leveraging modified SSO for centroid optimization leads to improved clustering accuracy. SSO significantly enhances the quality of initial cluster centers. These optimized starting points facilitate better convergence of the K-Means algorithm and consequently yield more accurate and representative clusters.
